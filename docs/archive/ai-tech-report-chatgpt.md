## Introduction: “Climate finance” as a newly stabilized economic object (1990–2025)

Between the early 1990s and 2025, *climate finance* moved from a loose policy phrase to a quasi-statistical object that is (i) **defined** (what counts), (ii) **classified** (instruments, actors, purposes), and (iii) **measured** (totals, baselines, mobilization effects, “progress” toward collective goals). This stabilization did not occur “outside” economics. It was co-produced by (a) economic institutions with statistical mandates (notably the OECD/DAC), (b) development finance organizations that embedded climate tags into lending and reporting systems (World Bank Group; MDBs), and (c) UNFCCC bodies that—under negotiation pressure—required commensurable aggregates (especially after Copenhagen’s USD 100bn goal and the creation of the Standing Committee on Finance, SCF). ([UNFCCC][1])

A history-of-economic-thought and methodology approach treats climate finance not primarily as “money out there,” but as a **quantified category** whose boundaries and calculation rules became sites of contestation and expertise. This aligns naturally with the historiography of quantification (Porter; Desrosières) and STS accounts of performativity (Callon; MacKenzie): numbers do not merely represent climate action; they format incentives, legitimacy, and bargaining positions, and they reshape what institutions do in order to be *countable*. ([JSTOR][2])

## 1. Institutional architecture and the making of commensurable totals

### 1.1 OECD/DAC: from development statistics to climate-tagged ODA (late 1990s–)

A central device in the emergence of climate finance as an object is the OECD/DAC **Creditor Reporting System (CRS)** with **Rio markers**. Introduced in 1998 for mitigation and later expanded with an adaptation marker (approved in late 2009; used for 2010 flows onward), the markers translated heterogeneous projects into standardized labels: *principal*, *significant*, or *not targeted*. ([wwflac.awsassets.panda.org][3])

Methodologically, the marker system institutionalized a distinctive epistemic compromise: rather than measure *incremental climate cost* in each project, it relies on donor reporting and codified judgement about objectives. This choice increased coverage and speed—crucial for diplomacy—but created predictable disputes about inflation, discretion, and comparability (discussed below).

### 1.2 World Bank Group and MDBs: internal tagging, joint methodologies, and “climate co-finance”

The multilateral development banks (MDBs), including the World Bank Group, developed their own tracking systems and—importantly—**harmonized** them through “joint” approaches and annual joint reporting. The 2015 Joint MDB Report is a key marker of consolidation, embedding common criteria (traceability to documentation; exclusion of non-quantifiable components) and detailed tracking methodologies (including adaptation finance identification and calculation). ([World Bank][4])

As MDB reporting became routinized, climate finance categories were increasingly welded to operational processes (project appraisal, results frameworks, portfolio targets). Recent World Bank climate disclosures illustrate how reporting is now tied to portfolio shares and institutional commitments, reinforcing the feedback loop between quantification and organizational strategy. ([The World Bank Docs][5])

### 1.3 UNFCCC bodies and the SCF: assessment as a negotiation technology (2010s–2020s)

Under the UNFCCC, the Copenhagen pledge and subsequent decisions created demand for an aggregate metric of “provided and mobilized” finance. Decision language emphasizes a “wide variety of sources, public and private, bilateral and multilateral,” which is expansive by design—and methodologically explosive. ([UNFCCC][1])

The SCF’s **Biennial Assessments and overviews of climate finance flows** became the focal synthesis arena, repeatedly stressing definitional plurality and measurement limits while still producing narrative and statistical overviews. SCF materials explicitly catalog the diversity of definitions and warn against false comparability—yet the very act of periodic assessment stabilizes the object by regularizing what must be counted and discussed. ([UNFCCC][6])

## 2. The economic categories that did the work

### 2.1 Instruments: grants, concessional loans, equity, guarantees

Climate finance debates borrowed heavily from development finance typologies: **grants** versus **loans**, public versus private channels, and the grey zone of blended instruments. The controversy is not only distributive (who pays) but methodological: instruments have different welfare and fiscal meanings, yet are often aggregated at **face value**.

A major methodological shift in adjacent development accounting was the DAC move to measure ODA loans on a **grant-equivalent** basis (standard from 2018), explicitly motivated by comparability and the need to reflect “donor effort” rather than gross flows. This reform became highly relevant to climate finance controversies because climate totals frequently include large loan volumes at face value. ([OECD][7])

### 2.2 Mobilized private finance, leverage, and attribution

From the mid-2010s, “mobilization” and “leverage” became indispensable categories—because public budgets alone could not plausibly meet political targets. The OECD developed methods for measuring **private finance mobilized** by official interventions, standardizing reporting approaches and embedding them in CRS-compatible frameworks. ([one.oecd.org][8])

Economically, this is where causal attribution becomes an accounting rule: *how much private money is credited to a public intervention?* The literature highlights recurring identification problems—counterfactual investment, multiple public actors claiming the same private deal, and inconsistent treatment of risk and guarantees.

## 3. Linkages with development economics, environmental economics, and negotiation expertise

### 3.1 Climate finance as an extension—and complication—of development finance

A consistent theme is that climate finance emerged by *reformatting* development finance rather than replacing it. Marker systems sit on top of ODA; MDB climate co-finance is carved out of broader development portfolios. This produces the “additionality” dilemma: climate finance must be both integrated (mainstreamed into development) and separable (countable as climate-specific support).

Work around additionality and baselines (often from policy-oriented research communities close to development studies and climate policy) shows how deeply “climate finance” depends on contested counterfactuals (additional to what? which year? which budget line?). Brown (ODI) is an early reference point framing additionality as a measurement and governance problem rather than a purely moral claim. ([ODI: Think change][9])

### 3.2 Expert economics inside negotiations: targets, trust, and auditability

Quantified targets (USD 100bn) converted finance into a credibility test in negotiations. OECD reports that operationalized “provided and mobilized” finance—especially around 2013–14—were not merely descriptive; they functioned as negotiation infrastructure, offering a calculative narrative of progress. ([OECD][10])

This is precisely where Porter’s “trust in numbers” logic applies: in low-trust political settings, standardized numbers are demanded as substitutes for interpersonal or diplomatic trust, even when everyone knows the numbers are fragile. ([JSTOR][2])

## 4. Core methodological and conceptual controversies (and what they reveal)

### 4.1 OECD vs NGOs: inflated totals, loans at face value, and the politics of definition

A large critical literature—often led by NGOs and academically affiliated researchers—argues that official totals overstate real support by counting loans at face value, including non-concessional lending, and relying on permissive definitions. Oxfam’s recurring “shadow reports” crystallize these critiques and explicitly propose re-estimation using grant-equivalent logic to approximate net support value. ([Oxfam Sverige][11])

### 4.2 Additionality: baseline pluralism as a structural, not accidental, feature

Additionality disputes persist because there is no neutral baseline: ODA trajectories, fiscal cycles, and “re-labelling” dynamics are endogenous to the target itself. The additionality debate thus exemplifies Desrosières’ point that statistical entities are partly conventions: the baseline is a negotiated convention that redistributes responsibility and credit. ([hup.harvard.edu][12])

### 4.3 Double counting and boundary problems (public/private; bilateral/multilateral)

Double counting problems recur across three interfaces:

* **Bilateral vs multilateral**: contributor countries count core contributions to MDBs/funds while MDBs count project-level climate finance.
* **Public vs private**: multiple public actors claim mobilization of the same private finance.
* **Mitigation vs adaptation; “dual benefits”**: mixed projects expose classification arbitrariness.

Weikmans & Roberts’ “accounting muddle” is a central synthesis of these controversies, showing how contestation is not peripheral but constitutive of the object. ([UNFCCC][13])

### 4.4 Private finance accounting: attribution, secrecy, and methodological fragility

Stadelmann, Michaelowa & Roberts (2013) remains a key reference on why private finance is systematically hard to account for in international climate policy: attribution rules, data opacity, and incentive-aligned over-claiming. The paper is also methodologically revealing: it shows how “mobilization” operates as both an econometric aspiration (causal impact) and an accounting convention (allocating credit). ([IDEAS/RePEc][14])

## 5. Situating the field: quantification, performativity, and climate economics

### 5.1 Quantification as governance (Desrosières; Porter)

The emergence of climate finance totals can be read as a textbook case of quantification in governance: categories (Rio markers; “mobilized private”) and aggregates (100bn progress) produce *legibility* at the cost of simplification. Desrosières helps frame how classification conventions become political technologies; Porter explains why numbers are demanded precisely where trust and accountability are strained. ([hup.harvard.edu][12])

### 5.2 Performativity: accounting rules shaping markets and organizations (Callon; MacKenzie)

From an STS angle, climate finance metrics are performative in at least two senses:

1. **Organizational performativity**: once portfolio targets and reporting frameworks exist, institutions re-engineer pipelines to produce countable “climate shares.”
2. **Market performativity**: blended finance and mobilization metrics encourage deal structures optimized for attributable private volumes.

Callon’s “laws of the markets” perspective and MacKenzie’s account of models shaping markets provide the conceptual bridge for treating climate finance accounting as a market- and institution-making practice, not mere description. ([Wiley Online Library][15])

### 5.3 Relationship to “history of climate economics”

Classic climate economics (carbon pricing, cost–benefit, integrated assessment) matters here less as theory than as **expertise ecology**: many finance categories translate economic reasoning about incentives and instruments into governance objects (e.g., concessionality, leverage). Yet the literature still too often treats finance measurement as technical housekeeping rather than as part of the evolution of climate economics’ policy apparatus.

## 7. Explicit gaps in the literature (1990–2025) and promising directions

1. **A genuine history of economic *classification work***: we have critiques of indicators, but fewer archival/organizational histories of how Rio markers and MDB taxonomies were negotiated internally (committees, training, audit routines, software).
2. **Micro-sociology of expertise in negotiations**: the SCF is studied mainly through outputs; we know less about how drafts, mandates, and expert networks translate disputes into publishable aggregates. ([UNFCCC][6])
3. **Grant-equivalent vs face-value as an epistemic rupture**: the DAC’s grant-equivalent reform is well documented for ODA, but its spillovers into climate finance accounting (and the political economy of resisting them) remain under-theorized. ([OECD][7])
4. **Performativity beyond markets**: STS work could be extended to show how accounting conventions reshape *project design* (what becomes “climate-tagged”), not only financial markets.
5. **South-led measurement perspectives**: much of the measurement architecture is provider-driven; the literature still lacks symmetrical accounts from recipient statistical systems and finance ministries (including how they contest, appropriate, or repurpose categories).
6. **Comparative regime interaction**: climate finance numbers increasingly intersect biodiversity, disaster risk, and SDG finance taxonomies; the historical literature is only beginning to map these collisions (and the resulting double-counting politics).

From 1990 to 2025, climate finance became an economic object through a dense interaction of development statistics, MDB operational accounting, and UNFCCC-driven synthesis under political targets. The object’s defining feature is not a settled definition, but a stabilized *field of calculative practices*—Rio markers, concessionality/grant-equivalent logics, and mobilization attribution rules—whose controversies are constitutive and performative rather than merely “noise.” ([UNFCCC][13])
