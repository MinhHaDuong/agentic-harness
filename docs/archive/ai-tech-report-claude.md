# Counting What Counts

## A Critical Literature Review on the Intellectual and Methodological Debates Surrounding Climate Finance in Economics and Political Economy

*For Historians of Economics and Economic Methodologists*


## Abstract

This literature review examines the intellectual and methodological controversies surrounding climate finance as a field of economic practice and scholarly inquiry. Tracing its emergence at the intersection of development economics, climate economics, and international political economy, the review analyzes how economists and international institutions have shaped both the definition and measurement of climate finance. Particular attention is given to disputes over OECD accounting methodologies, Rio markers, grant-equivalent versus face-value reporting, and the attribution of mobilized private finance. The review examines ongoing debates about additionality, the crowding-out of development aid, and the contested boundary between public and private finance. By mapping disagreements between institutions (OECD, UNFCCC, multilateral development banks) and civil society actors (Oxfam, CARE, Third World Network), the analysis reveals how these technical disputes reflect deeper theoretical assumptions within economics about market efficiency, public goods provision, and global distributive justice. The review concludes by identifying unresolved conceptual tensions and under-explored historical questions relevant for historians of economic thought.


## 1. Introduction: The Problem of Definition

Climate finance presents a paradox familiar to historians of economic thought: a concept that is simultaneously indispensable to global policy discourse and fundamentally contested in its meaning. Since the 2009 Copenhagen Accord committed developed countries to mobilize $100 billion annually by 2020 for developing nations, the question of what counts as climate finance has become a site of intense methodological and political struggle. Yet unlike many concepts in economics whose definitions stabilized through professional consensus, climate finance remains stubbornly plural in its meanings, reflecting unresolved tensions between competing theoretical frameworks, institutional interests, and normative commitments.

The UNFCCC Standing Committee on Finance offers perhaps the closest thing to an official definition, describing climate finance as resources that *aim at reducing emissions and enhancing sinks of greenhouse gases* and *reducing vulnerability of, and maintaining and increasing the resilience of, human and ecological systems to negative climate change impacts*. Yet this capacious formulation obscures rather than resolves the fundamental measurement questions that have animated scholarly and policy debate. As Development Initiatives has observed, when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure—an application of Goodhart's Law that has proven particularly apt for climate finance accounting.

This review examines the scholarly and policy literature on climate finance with an eye toward questions that should interest historians of economics and economic methodologists. How did climate finance emerge as a distinct object of economic analysis? What role have economists played as experts in defining its boundaries? How do contemporary measurement controversies reflect deeper theoretical assumptions about markets, states, and global distribution? And what can these debates tell us about the relationship between economic knowledge and political power in international governance?


## 2. Historical Emergence: Development Economics Meets Climate Policy

### 2.1 The Rio Framework and Inherited Institutional Infrastructure

Climate finance did not emerge ex nihilo at Copenhagen in 2009. Its conceptual and institutional foundations were laid at the 1992 Earth Summit in Rio de Janeiro, where the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change established the principle that developed countries *shall provide* financial resources to assist developing countries with climate action. Article 4.3 of the UNFCCC stipulated that such finance should be *new and additional*—language that would prove consequential and contentious in subsequent decades.

Crucially, the UNFCCC inherited rather than created the institutional infrastructure for tracking international financial flows. The OECD's Development Assistance Committee (DAC), established in 1961 to coordinate aid policies among wealthy nations, already operated a Creditor Reporting System (CRS) that tracked bilateral aid flows. When the DAC introduced its "Rio markers" in 1998 to identify development finance targeting the objectives of the Rio Conventions, it effectively grafted climate finance accounting onto an existing system designed for different purposes. This institutional inheritance has shaped climate finance measurement ever since, creating what scholars have described as an "additional layer of complexity" arising from the overlap between climate finance and development assistance reporting systems.

### 2.2 The Copenhagen Watershed and the $100 Billion Target

The 2009 Copenhagen Accord marked a transformation in climate finance discourse. For the first time, developed countries committed to a specific quantitative target: mobilizing $100 billion annually by 2020 for developing countries' climate needs. This figure emerged not from systematic needs assessments but from political negotiations—an origin that has shaped subsequent debates about its adequacy. The Cancun Agreements of 2010 formalized this commitment while adding criteria that finance should be "balanced" between mitigation and adaptation, and that the private sector could be considered a source of climate finance.

The introduction of a quantitative target transformed climate finance from an abstract commitment into a measurable objective, with profound consequences for institutional practice. As scholars have observed, this transformation invited precisely the kind of target-chasing behavior that Goodhart's Law predicts: creative accounting, definitional stretching, and strategic reclassification of existing flows. The Danish development economist Charles Kenny has argued that evidence suggests donors are "considerably stripping aid finance from development projects to support climate projects," a claim that speaks directly to the additionality concerns embedded in the original UNFCCC framework.

### 2.3 The Paris Agreement and Evolving Governance

The 2015 Paris Agreement introduced additional complexity by establishing a "New Collective Quantified Goal" (NCQG) to succeed the $100 billion target after 2025, while simultaneously broadening the climate finance agenda to encompass adaptation, loss and damage, and the Article 2.1(c) objective of making "finance flows consistent with a pathway towards low greenhouse gas emissions and climate-resilient development." This evolution reflects what political economists have described as a "broadening out from a mitigation focus under Kyoto to a triple goal comprising mitigation, adaptation, and finance under Paris"—a shift with significant implications for how climate finance is conceptualized and measured.


## 3. Economists as Policy Experts: Epistemic Authority and Definitional Power

The role of economists in shaping climate finance definitions deserves particular attention from historians of economic thought. Unlike many domains where economic expertise emerged gradually through academic institutionalization, climate finance was constituted from its inception as a site of technocratic authority, with economists embedded in international organizations wielding significant influence over what counts and how.

### 3.1 The OECD and Technical Standard-Setting

The OECD has emerged as the dominant institution for climate finance accounting, a position that reflects both its historical role in development finance statistics and the absence of clear measurement guidelines in the Paris Agreement itself. As scholars have noted, "given that the Paris Agreement did not specify how climate finance should be measured, or provide any definition suitable for practical measurement, countries had no official guidance on which to base their reporting. The existence of the Rio markers filled this vacuum." This institutional default has profound implications: the categories, methodologies, and professional norms of development economics have been imported wholesale into climate finance measurement.

The economists staffing the OECD DAC have made consequential choices about measurement methodology that embed particular theoretical assumptions. The decision to report loans at face value rather than grant equivalent, the creation of "principal" and "significant" Rio marker categories, the methodology for attributing "mobilized" private finance—each of these technical choices reflects contestable judgments about what climate finance is for and how its adequacy should be assessed. Yet these choices are often presented as merely technical, obscuring their normative dimensions.

### 3.2 Competing Frameworks: Welfare Economics Versus Political Economy

The literature reveals a fundamental tension between two theoretical frameworks for understanding climate finance. The first, rooted in welfare economics and public goods theory, conceptualizes climate finance as a mechanism for addressing global externalities. From this perspective, climate mitigation represents a global public good whose benefits are non-excludable and non-rival; the challenge is designing institutions that overcome free-rider problems to ensure efficient provision. Nicholas Stern's influential framing of climate change as "the greatest market failure the world has ever seen" exemplifies this approach, which emphasizes cost-effectiveness, leverage ratios, and the mobilization of private capital.

The second framework, drawing on political economy and theories of global justice, emphasizes the historical responsibilities of wealthy nations and the principle that polluters should pay. This approach treats climate finance not as charitable assistance but as compensation owed to victims of an injustice. The principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities" (CBDR-RC), enshrined in the UNFCCC, reflects this normative foundation. As Mizan Khan has argued, the Polluter Pays Principle "reflects the most fundamental principles of justice and responsibility"—a framing that implies very different measurement priorities than efficiency-focused approaches.

These competing frameworks generate different answers to fundamental measurement questions. Should loans count the same as grants? Should private finance mobilized by public guarantees count toward developed country commitments? Should adaptation and mitigation be weighted equally? The technical debates examined below can be understood as proxy conflicts between these underlying theoretical positions.


## 4. Measurement Controversies: Technical Disputes with Theoretical Stakes

### 4.1 The Rio Markers System

The OECD DAC Rio markers constitute the primary methodology for tracking bilateral climate finance, yet their application has generated persistent controversy. The system classifies development finance activities as having climate change mitigation or adaptation as either a "principal" objective (score of 2), "significant" objective (score of 1), or not targeted (score of 0). Activities marked as "principal" are typically counted at 100% of their value, while "significant" activities are subject to varying coefficients across reporting countries.

Scholars have identified multiple problems with this system. First, the distinction between "principal" and "significant" involves considerable subjectivity. The OECD handbook acknowledges that "the methodology is based on the purpose of the activity and not the type of activity," but purpose is often ambiguous and susceptible to strategic framing. Research using machine learning analysis of project descriptions has found that "significant-marked projects are most likely to be wrongly categorised," suggesting systematic inflation of climate relevance.

Second, reporting practices vary dramatically across countries. Development Initiatives' research found that "the degree of correspondence between the indicative table and countries' markings" varied substantially, with France and Japan showing particularly poor alignment with OECD guidance. Studies employing natural language processing have corroborated these findings, suggesting that some major donors' climate finance reporting is substantially less reliable than others'.

Third, there is no common standard for converting Rio marker data into climate-specific figures reported to the UNFCCC. Countries apply different coefficients to "significant" marked activities (ranging from 0% to 100%), use different methodologies for attributing multilateral contributions, and report on different bases (commitments versus disbursements). This heterogeneity undermines comparability and aggregate reliability.

### 4.2 Face Value Versus Grant Equivalent

Perhaps no measurement controversy has generated more sustained criticism than the practice of reporting loans at face value rather than grant equivalent. The distinction is straightforward: a $100 million loan that must be repaid with interest represents a fundamentally different financial transfer than a $100 million grant. Yet the OECD's climate finance reports have traditionally counted both at face value, a methodology that critics argue systematically overstates developed country contributions.

Oxfam's Climate Finance Shadow Reports have been particularly influential in articulating this critique. Their methodology calculates the "Climate-Specific Net Assistance" (CSNA) by converting reported finance to grant-equivalent terms using the OECD's own Differentiated Discount Rates. The results are striking: Oxfam estimates that the "true value" of climate finance in 2022 was between $28-35 billion, compared to the $116 billion reported by the OECD. This discrepancy—roughly a factor of three—reflects primarily the dominance of loans in climate finance provision: loans constituted approximately 70% of reported public climate finance in recent years.

The face value versus grant equivalent debate has significant implications for development economics. Research by Oxfam and CARE has found that developing countries are now "paying more back to wealthy nations for climate finance loans than they receive—for every 5 dollars they receive they are paying 7 dollars back." This finding inverts the conventional understanding of climate finance as North-South transfer, suggesting instead a mechanism that may be exacerbating developing country debt burdens. France, Japan, and Italy have been identified as particularly reliant on loan-based climate finance, while Nordic countries tend to provide higher shares of grants.

### 4.3 Mobilized Private Finance

The Cancun Agreements established private finance as a legitimate component of the $100 billion commitment, opening a further methodological frontier. The question of how to attribute private investment to public interventions has proven conceptually and empirically challenging. As the UNFCCC Standing Committee on Finance has acknowledged, "measurement and reporting of mobilised private finance continues to be complex and difficult."

The theoretical foundations for counting mobilized private finance rest on the concept of "leverage"—the idea that public finance can catalyze private investment that would not otherwise occur. This concept draws on mainstream finance theory about risk mitigation and the role of public guarantees in correcting market failures. Yet critics have questioned both the empirical reality of claimed leverage ratios and their normative appropriateness for assessing developed country climate commitments.

Empirically, evidence suggests that claims about private finance mobilization have often been exaggerated. Research by UNCTAD has described "a longer history of exaggerated expectations about the leverage ratios of private capital." The UNFCCC Standing Committee on Finance reports that "private finance mobilized by public finance and attributed to developed countries dropped by 2 per cent in 2019 compared to 2018 and by a further 9 per cent in 2020 relative to 2019"—contrary to expectations of rapid scaling.

Normatively, developing countries have frequently objected to private finance counting toward developed country commitments, particularly for adaptation. As scholars have noted, "developing countries have often opposed private finance, especially for adaptation, noting that public finance can be better targeted to areas of need." This objection reflects the justice-based framing of climate finance discussed above: if developed countries owe compensation for historical emissions, that obligation should not be dischargeable through private investment decisions over which neither debtors nor creditors have direct control.


## 5. Additionality and the Public-Private Boundary

### 5.1 The Additionality Debate

The requirement that climate finance be "new and additional," enshrined in the UNFCCC since 1992, has generated one of the most persistent debates in the field. At stake is whether climate finance represents genuinely additional resources for developing countries or merely relabeled development assistance diverted from other priorities.

The conceptual challenge is that "new and additional" lacks an agreed definition. Scholars have identified at least four prominent interpretations in policy debates: climate finance as additional to the 0.7% ODA target; as an increase on 2009 levels of climate-related ODA; as a specified limited percentage of ODA; or as an entirely separate funding stream disconnected from ODA. Each definition implies different measurement requirements and yields different assessments of whether commitments have been met.

Empirical research suggests grounds for concern about additionality. Analysis by the Center for Global Development found that "total ODA delivered to developing countries from members of the OECD DAC, excluding humanitarian support, was $56 billion in 2018, $53 billion in 2015, and $51 billion in 2008"—a pattern suggesting "that aid flowing under the umbrella of climate finance isn't in fact additional, it is substituting for other aid or has been relabeled as climate finance." Similar findings by ODI indicate that approximately 45% of reported climate finance may not meet even minimal standards of additionality.

The additionality debate intersects with broader questions in development economics about aid effectiveness and fungibility. If climate finance diverts resources from health, education, or productive investment, the net welfare impact on developing countries could be negative even if nominal climate finance targets are met. As Inge Kaul has argued, public finance for global public goods like climate mitigation should ideally be separated from ODA entirely, to improve transparency about what is actually being provided for which purposes.

### 5.2 Crowding Out Development Aid

Related to additionality is the question of whether climate finance is crowding out other forms of development assistance. Research by IIED has found that "international public climate finance is growing, particularly to support mitigation in middle-income countries, and there is some evidence that it is crowding out finance to meet the SDGs, particularly in LDCs, where aid is falling." This geographic distribution raises justice concerns: climate mitigation finance tends to flow to middle-income countries with larger emissions reduction potential, while the least developed countries most vulnerable to climate impacts receive proportionally less.

Oxfam's analysis has consistently highlighted this distributional pattern. Their research indicates that only about 20% of reported climate finance reaches Least Developed Countries, with just 3% going to Small Island Developing States—despite these nations facing the gravest climate threats with the fewest resources to cope. This finding suggests a disconnect between climate finance allocation and climate vulnerability, reflecting perhaps the influence of mitigation-focused efficiency criteria over adaptation-focused equity considerations.

### 5.3 The Public-Private Boundary

The boundary between public and private finance has become increasingly blurred in climate finance discourse, with significant implications for accountability. Multilateral development banks occupy an ambiguous position: they leverage public capital (including callable capital from member states) to borrow on private markets and lend to developing countries. Their climate finance is typically counted toward the $100 billion goal, yet the degree to which this represents genuine public commitment versus commercial banking activity is contested.

Research on MDB climate finance has highlighted concerns about concessionality and transparency. Analysis by the World Resources Institute found that "both the share and absolute amount of MDB climate financing as grants decreased from 10% ($6.08 billion) in 2022 to 6.7% ($4.98 billion) in 2023"—a concerning trend given that "three-fifths of low-income economies are at risk of, or are already in, debt distress." Unlike bilateral donors, MDBs are not required to report the terms of their loans to the DAC, making assessment of their true concessionality difficult.

The concept of "blended finance," which combines public concessional resources with private capital, further complicates the public-private boundary. Proponents argue that blending allows scarce public resources to catalyze larger private flows. Critics contend that it represents a privatization of development finance that shifts risk to public actors while allowing private actors to capture returns from climate investments. This debate mirrors broader controversies in development economics about the appropriate role of private capital in addressing public goods deficits.


## 6. Institutional Conflicts and Civil Society Critiques

### 6.1 OECD Versus UNFCCC: Competing Authorities

The institutional landscape of climate finance governance reveals significant tensions between the OECD and UNFCCC frameworks. While the OECD has emerged as the de facto standard-setter for climate finance accounting, developing countries have consistently pushed for greater UNFCCC authority, viewing the OECD as a "rich country club" whose methodologies favor donor interests.

The UNFCCC Standing Committee on Finance has attempted to establish its own definitional authority, producing biennial assessments and proposing definitions that emphasize alignment with nationally determined contributions and national adaptation plans. Yet the Committee's capacity to impose binding standards remains limited. As the Grantham Research Institute has observed, "the absence of clear and consistent criteria for defining international climate finance under the UNFCCC" creates space for continued reporting heterogeneity.

This institutional competition reflects deeper geopolitical tensions. The division between Annex II (developed) and non-Annex I (developing) countries established in 1992 has become increasingly contested, with developed countries seeking to expand the contributor base to include emerging economies like China and Gulf states, while developing countries resist what they view as burden-shifting. The NCQG negotiations have become a focal point for these struggles over climate finance governance.

### 6.2 Civil Society Counter-Accounting

International NGOs have developed sophisticated counter-accounting methodologies that challenge official figures. Oxfam's Climate Finance Shadow Reports, published annually since 2020, have been particularly influential in demonstrating the gap between reported and "true" climate finance. Their methodology—converting loans to grant equivalent, applying more conservative climate relevance coefficients, and excluding dubious projects—consistently yields figures one-third to one-quarter of official totals.

CARE International, often in partnership with Oxfam through its Climate Justice Centre, has contributed additional analyses emphasizing the gender dimensions of climate finance and the particular needs of adaptation funding. Their research has highlighted that "only 3% of finance specifically aimed at enhancing gender equality, despite the climate crisis disproportionately impacting women and girls"—a finding that extends the critique of climate finance beyond aggregate totals to distributional questions.

The Third World Network, representing developing country civil society perspectives, has articulated a more fundamental critique rooted in climate justice principles. Their analysis emphasizes the historical responsibilities of developed countries and frames climate finance as compensation rather than charity. As Meenakshi Raman has written, developed countries "continued to dilute their responsibilities and evade their obligations, particularly in the critical area of climate finance—undermining trust and jeopardising the prospects for equitable global climate action."

These civil society interventions represent an important form of epistemic contestation. By producing alternative numbers grounded in different normative premises, NGOs challenge the apparent objectivity of official statistics and demonstrate the irreducibly political character of climate finance measurement. This dynamic echoes earlier episodes in the history of economic statistics, where social movements have contested official measures of unemployment, poverty, or national income.


## 7. Theoretical Underpinnings: Economics and Its Assumptions

### 7.1 Market Efficiency and the De-Risking Paradigm

The dominant approach to climate finance in mainstream economics and multilateral institutions emphasizes "de-risking" private investment through public guarantees, blended finance, and policy reforms. This paradigm rests on assumptions about market efficiency: that private capital allocation is generally optimal, and that the role of public finance is to correct specific market failures (information asymmetries, externalities, risk concentration) that prevent efficient private flows.

Political economists have challenged this framework on multiple grounds. As recent scholarship in the Review of International Political Economy has argued, "an approach which focuses only on using public funds to lever and de-risk private ones is only addressing half the problem: the question of mobilising finance rather than regulating and redistributing it which is equally pressing to divert finance away from fossil fuels." The de-risking paradigm may improve efficiency at the margin while leaving structural features of global financial inequality untouched.

The emphasis on leverage ratios—the amount of private capital mobilized per dollar of public investment—illustrates how efficiency metrics can displace equity considerations. A high leverage ratio may indicate effective use of public resources from a market perspective, yet it tells us nothing about whether the resulting investments reach those most in need or address the most pressing climate vulnerabilities. Current leverage ratios for MDB climate finance are often less than one, suggesting limited private mobilization despite rhetorical emphasis on private capital.

### 7.2 Public Goods and Global Commons

Climate economics draws heavily on public goods theory to frame the rationale for climate finance. Atmospheric stability is a paradigmatic global public good: non-excludable (no one can be prevented from benefiting from a stable climate) and non-rival (one person's benefit does not diminish another's). Standard economic theory predicts under-provision of such goods by private markets, justifying public intervention.

Yet the application of public goods theory to climate finance raises difficulties that the literature has not fully resolved. Mitigation finance—investment in emissions reduction—produces global benefits regardless of where it occurs. Should such investment in developing countries count as climate finance owed by developed countries, or simply as efficient global public goods provision that benefits everyone equally? As scholars have noted, "the global benefit is the same whether the wind farm is built in a developed country like Australia or whether it is built in a developing country."

Adaptation finance presents different theoretical challenges. Unlike mitigation, adaptation benefits are largely local—building sea walls in Bangladesh protects Bangladeshis, not Germans. This local character undermines the global public goods rationale for developed country financing of developing country adaptation. Yet justice-based arguments suggest that those who caused climate change should pay for adaptation by those who suffer its consequences, regardless of whether adaptation constitutes a public good in the technical sense.

### 7.3 The Polluter Pays Principle and Historical Responsibility

An alternative theoretical foundation for climate finance derives from the Polluter Pays Principle (PPP), an economic concept that originated in environmental economics but has implications for climate justice. The PPP holds that those who cause pollution should bear the costs of remediation or compensation, internalizing externalities and providing efficient incentives for emissions reduction.

Applying the PPP to climate change requires grappling with historical emissions—the accumulated stock of greenhouse gases resulting from two centuries of industrial development concentrated in wealthy nations. Scholars like Mizan Khan have argued that the UNFCCC principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities" implicitly recognizes the PPP: developed countries bear greater obligations because they caused more of the problem. Yet the UNFCCC notably did not explicitly incorporate the PPP, a lacuna that continues to shape debates about climate finance obligations.

The PPP raises difficulties for intergenerational justice that economists have struggled to address. Present-day citizens of developed countries did not personally cause historical emissions; present-day citizens of developing countries are not the same individuals who will suffer future climate impacts. Some scholars have proposed a "forward-looking polluter pays principle" that focuses on future emissions, combined with an "ability to pay" principle for historical responsibility. Others argue that the benefits received from historical emissions—the accumulated wealth of developed countries—create present-day obligations regardless of individual responsibility.


## 8. Unresolved Conceptual Tensions

This review has identified several conceptual tensions that remain unresolved in the scholarly literature and policy discourse on climate finance.

### 8.1 Efficiency Versus Equity

The tension between efficiency and equity criteria runs throughout climate finance debates. Efficiency considerations suggest directing mitigation finance where emissions reductions are cheapest—typically middle-income countries with large industrial sectors. Equity considerations suggest prioritizing the most vulnerable countries and communities. The dominance of mitigation over adaptation in climate finance flows (approximately 2:1 in recent years) reflects the influence of efficiency logic, yet this allocation is difficult to justify on equity grounds.

### 8.2 Quantity Versus Quality

The focus on aggregate targets ($100 billion, $300 billion) has drawn attention away from qualitative dimensions of climate finance. The composition of finance—grants versus loans, mitigation versus adaptation, bilateral versus multilateral—may matter as much as quantity for recipient countries' ability to address climate challenges. Yet the political economy of international negotiations favors quantitative targets that can be easily measured and communicated, even when such targets incentivize quantity over quality.

### 8.3 Integration Versus Separation

The relationship between climate finance and development finance remains conceptually unsettled. Integration offers potential synergies: climate-resilient development can advance both agendas simultaneously. Yet integration also creates opportunities for relabeling and burden-shifting, undermining the additionality principle. Separation would provide clearer accountability but might miss opportunities for mainstreaming climate considerations into development practice. The literature has not resolved this tension, though the empirical evidence of crowding out suggests that integration without additionality safeguards poses real risks.

### 8.4 National Versus Global Framing

Climate finance governance operates within a Westphalian framework of nation-states, yet climate change is fundamentally a global phenomenon that transcends national boundaries. The literature increasingly recognizes what scholars term "cross-border" or "borderless" climate risks that cannot be addressed through territorially bounded adaptation. This conceptual mismatch suggests the need for new governance frameworks, yet the institutional infrastructure for genuinely global climate finance remains underdeveloped.


## 9. Under-Explored Historical Questions

For historians of economic thought, the climate finance debates surveyed here suggest several under-explored research questions.

### 9.1 Intellectual Origins

The intellectual genealogy of climate finance concepts deserves systematic historical investigation. How did the concept of "mobilized" private finance enter climate discourse? What was the role of particular economists or economic institutions in shaping the $100 billion figure? How did the Rio markers methodology evolve from aid tracking to climate finance measurement? Archival research on UNFCCC negotiations, OECD deliberations, and World Bank internal debates could illuminate the contingent choices that created present-day accounting frameworks.

### 9.2 The Development-Climate Nexus

The relationship between climate economics and development economics deserves more attention from historians. Climate finance emerged in part by borrowing institutional infrastructure, professional norms, and theoretical frameworks from development economics. Yet it also represents a challenge to development paradigms focused on growth and poverty reduction. How have development economists responded to climate finance? Has climate change transformed development economics, or has development economics captured climate finance within its existing frameworks?

### 9.3 The Role of Economists in International Organizations

The exercise of epistemic authority by economists in the OECD, World Bank, and other international organizations presents opportunities for research in the history of economic expertise. How have economists in these institutions understood their role in climate finance debates? What professional networks and career incentives shape their approach? How have they navigated tensions between technical expertise and political pressures from member states? Case studies of particular economists or institutional units could illuminate the micro-foundations of climate finance governance.

### 9.4 Civil Society and Alternative Expertise

The development of counter-accounting by NGOs like Oxfam represents an interesting case of expertise outside the academy. How did these organizations develop their methodological capacity? What is the relationship between their technical work and their advocacy objectives? How have official institutions responded to civil society challenges to their numbers? This history could contribute to broader literatures on the democratization of economic expertise and the politics of statistics.

### 9.5 Comparative Histories of Environmental Finance

Climate finance did not emerge in isolation but as part of broader developments in environmental finance, sustainable finance, and green bonds. Comparative historical analysis could situate climate finance within this larger field. How do the measurement controversies in climate finance compare to those in biodiversity finance or sustainable development finance? What can the history of environmental accounting more broadly teach us about the prospects for resolving current climate finance disputes?


## 10. Conclusion

Climate finance occupies a peculiar position in contemporary economic discourse: simultaneously a technical accounting exercise and a site of fundamental normative contestation. The measurement controversies examined in this review—over Rio markers, grant equivalents, mobilized private finance, and additionality—are not merely technical disputes awaiting resolution through better methodology. They reflect irreducible conflicts between competing theoretical frameworks, institutional interests, and visions of global justice.

For historians of economics, climate finance offers a contemporary laboratory for studying the relationship between economic knowledge and political power. The OECD's dominance in standard-setting, the contest between efficiency and equity frameworks, the role of civil society counter-expertise—these dynamics echo earlier episodes in the history of economic statistics while presenting distinctive features shaped by the global scale and urgency of the climate crisis.

The unresolved tensions identified in this review—between efficiency and equity, quantity and quality, integration and separation, national and global framings—are unlikely to be resolved through technical refinement alone. They require political choices about what climate finance is for and who should bear its costs. Economists can inform these choices, but they cannot make them on purely scientific grounds. Recognizing this limitation may be the most important contribution that historians of economic thought can make to ongoing climate finance debates.

As the world moves from the $100 billion target to the New Collective Quantified Goal and beyond, the stakes of these measurement controversies will only increase. The scholarly community—including historians attentive to how economic concepts acquire authority and consequence—has an important role to play in ensuring that climate finance debates proceed with appropriate awareness of their theoretical assumptions and political implications.
